Jannik Sinner, the reigning Wimbledon men's singles champion, faced a stern test in his first-round match against Miomir Kecmanovic, ultimately securing a hard-fought five-set victory. The top-ranked Italian battled back from being down a set twice and recovered from a concerning fall on the grass. This challenging start to his title defense on Centre Court highlighted the pressure and unique demands of opening the prestigious tournament. His resilience in this 3-hour, 30-minute encounter sets the tone for his campaign, especially with main rival Carlos Alcaraz absent due to injury.

## What happened
Jannik Sinner, the defending Wimbledon champion and world No. 1, began his 2026 campaign with a demanding 4-6, 6-3, 6-7 (6), 6-2, 6-3 victory over 50th-ranked Miomir Kecmanovic. The match, lasting 3 hours and 30 minutes, saw Sinner come back from a set down twice, marking his first grass-court match of the season. He admitted to feeling "a little tight in the beginning" and also had a worrisome fall on the grass early in the third set, though he quickly resumed playing and later downplayed the injury as "just a nail."

Sinner displayed moments of brilliance, including a perfectly executed sliced stop-volley drop shot, but also struggled with unforced errors, tallying 52 compared to Kecmanovic's 33. Despite this, his offensive power was evident with 72 winners and 31 aces. The third set proved particularly challenging, with Kecmanovic saving a set point in the tiebreaker before Sinner's backhand sailed long, giving his opponent a two-sets-to-one lead. However, Sinner "cleaned up his game" in the final two sets to secure the win, maintaining his perfect 5-0 record against Kecmanovic.

## Why it matters
This opening match was a significant test for Sinner, not just physically but mentally, following his recent struggles with dizziness at the French Open. Overcoming a two-sets-to-one deficit on Centre Court, a stage reserved for the defending champion, demonstrates his ability to perform under immense pressure and regain composure. Historically, only one men's singles champion in the professional era, Lleyton Hewitt in 2003, has lost in the first round the following year, underscoring the weight of expectation on Sinner.

The victory is crucial for Sinner's confidence and momentum, especially given that his main rival, Carlos Alcaraz, is absent from the championships due to injury. This absence positions Sinner as a strong favorite to repeat as champion, making a shaky but ultimately successful start a valuable experience. His ability to adapt and improve his game over five sets, despite early nerves and a physical scare, signals his readiness for the challenges ahead in the tournament.
