On day seven of Wimbledon, tennis titan Novak Djokovic encountered an uncharacteristically tough opening from qualifier Roman Safiullin, who pushed the multi-Grand Slam champion to a first-set tie-break. This early challenge underscored the unpredictable nature of Grand Slam tennis, demonstrating that even the most dominant players can be tested by determined underdogs. Djokovic, seeking to extend his record-breaking Grand Slam quarter-final appearances, had to dig deep and adjust his game to navigate the initial pressure from the unseeded Russian. The match served as a stark reminder that every point at Wimbledon is fiercely contested, regardless of ranking.

## What happened
Roman Safiullin, the qualifier, demonstrated remarkable resilience and tactical play in the opening set against Novak Djokovic. He managed to take the first set to a tie-break, a significant feat against a player of Djokovic's caliber. Safiullin notably adopted a deep position behind the baseline for Djokovic's first serves, a strategy that, while potentially aimed at staying cool in the shade, also influenced the flow of play.

Djokovic, known for his adaptability, responded by increasingly employing a serve-and-volley tactic, which proved effective in the latter part of the first set and throughout the second. Despite Safiullin initially winning many of the longer rallies, Djokovic's adjustments, particularly his serve-and-volley plays, allowed him to secure the first set in the tie-break and then calmly serve out the second set, moving two sets to love ahead.
