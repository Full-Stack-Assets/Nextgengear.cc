A federal appeals court has delivered a major blow to the Trump administration's efforts to obtain confidential voter registration data from states. The 6th US Circuit Court of Appeals ruled that Michigan is not obligated to provide non-public voter information, such as Social Security and driver's license numbers, to the Justice Department. This decision marks the first time an appeals court has weighed in on the administration's sweeping campaign for unredacted voter rolls, potentially paving the way for a Supreme Court review. It underscores a broader judicial pushback against the administration's attempts to assert greater federal control over elections, fueled by claims of a rigged 2020 election.

<Callout type="takeaway">A federal appeals court has significantly curbed the Trump Justice Department's ability to demand confidential state voter data, asserting the administration's interpretation of a key civil rights law is flawed.</Callout>

## What happened

The 6th US Circuit Court of Appeals upheld a lower court's decision, stating that Michigan was not required to hand over confidential voter registration files to the Justice Department. This data could include sensitive personal identifiers like Social Security numbers and driver's license IDs. This ruling represents the first appellate-level rejection of the Trump administration's nationwide campaign to audit unredacted voter rolls, a campaign that has already faced opposition from nine district court judges.

Circuit Judge Andre Mathis, writing for the majority, clarified that the 1960 Civil Rights Act, which the Justice Department cited, does not apply to voter registration files created and maintained internally by states. The court noted that Michigan's Secretary of State did not "acquire, obtain, or receive" the qualified voter file from a third party, thus falling outside the scope of the law intended to combat Jim Crow-era voter discrimination. The Justice Department had also failed to meet other legal requirements for its data requests.

The administration's stated purpose for seeking these voter files has evolved, but more recently, it has indicated plans to compare state voter rolls against a Department of Homeland Security system for citizenship verification. This DHS tool has been criticized for generating false positives, particularly affecting naturalized citizens who might be wrongly flagged as ineligible voters due to outdated data.

## Why it matters

This appeals court decision carries significant weight, as it challenges the Trump administration's expansive interpretation of federal power in election oversight. By limiting the Justice Department's ability to compel states to surrender sensitive voter data, the ruling protects individual privacy and state autonomy in election administration. It also sets a precedent that could influence ongoing litigation in other states where the DOJ has sued for similar information, potentially leading to a Supreme Court clash over the scope of federal intervention in state elections.

The ruling is particularly relevant given the administration's repeated assertions of a "rigged" 2020 election, which has fueled its efforts to exert more federal control over voting processes. Judicial pushback, as seen in this and other recent rulings blocking the use of federal citizenship data for purging voter rolls or striking down parts of a 2025 executive order on election rules, indicates a strong judicial branch resistance to these federal overreaches. The outcome impacts not only state election officials who would be forced to comply with broad data demands but also voters whose confidential information could be subjected to potentially flawed verification processes.

## How to think about it

This ruling highlights the ongoing tension between federal oversight and state control in election administration. When evaluating such developments, it's crucial to consider the balance between ensuring election integrity and protecting voter privacy and state sovereignty. While the desire to maintain accurate voter rolls is legitimate, the methods employed must respect established legal frameworks and avoid overreaching interpretations of existing laws.
