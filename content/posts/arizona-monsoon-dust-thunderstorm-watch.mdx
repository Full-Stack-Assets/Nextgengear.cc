A monsoon surge is stirring up dust and thunderstorms across the Phoenix metro this weekend. A Blowing Dust Advisory takes effect this evening, followed by a Severe Thunderstorm Watch that lasts until 10 p.m. Sunday brings a First Alert Day with a 40 % chance of showers and possible severe storms. Residents should prepare for reduced visibility and gusty winds as outflow boundaries shift. Understanding the timing and intensity of these events can help protect health, property, and daily plans.

## What happened
The National Weather Service has issued a Blowing Dust Advisory for Pinal County and southern Maricopa County from 6 p.m. to 10 p.m., with a Severe Thunderstorm Watch in effect for the same region until 10 p.m. Scattered thunderstorms are already moving across southeastern and north‑central Arizona, bringing heavy rain, strong wind gusts, and pockets of blowing dust that could reduce visibility in places like Queen Creek.

Sunday is forecast as a First Alert Day. A 40 % chance of showers and thunderstorms is expected to develop late in the afternoon and persist into the evening, with the potential for a few storms to become severe, producing damaging wind gusts. Rainfall amounts could range from 0.10 to 0.25 inches, while afternoon highs hover around 109 °F.

The monsoon pattern will linger into the following week, with a 20 % chance of showers on Monday and continued chances of rain through Wednesday. Temperatures will stay near 108 °F through Wednesday, dip to about 105 °F on Thursday, and fall into the upper 90s to around 100 °F by the weekend.

## Why it matters
The dust advisory threatens air quality and driving safety, especially for commuters in affected counties. Strong outflow winds can topple unsecured objects and damage structures, while heavy rain raises flash‑flood concerns in low‑lying areas. For residents and businesses, the combination of reduced visibility and severe wind gusts means heightened risk to health, property, and daily operations.

## How to think about it
Plan outdoor activities for early morning or later evening when dust and wind are lower. Keep windows and doors closed during peak dust hours, and use air purifiers if you have respiratory sensitivities. Secure loose items in your yard or on balconies before storms arrive, and monitor local alerts for real‑time updates on wind gusts and flash‑flood warnings.

<Callout type="warning">Do not drive through dust‑filled roads or ignore severe thunderstorm warnings; the combination can lead to dangerous conditions and sudden damage.</Callout>
