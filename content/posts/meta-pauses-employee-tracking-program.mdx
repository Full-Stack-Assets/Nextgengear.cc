Meta has paused its employee-tracking program after an internal security issue exposed sensitive data to other workers. The program, known as the Model Compatibility Initiative, collected data such as mouse movements and keystrokes from employees. The pause comes after employees raised concerns about the program's impact on their privacy and security. Meta executives had defended the program, saying it was necessary to train AI systems. However, the internal security issue has led to a re-evaluation of the program's effectiveness. 
   <Callout type="takeaway"> Meta's employee-tracking program has been paused due to an internal data leak, highlighting concerns over employee privacy and security.</Callout>
   ## What happened
   Meta rolled out the Model Compatibility Initiative tool in April to US employees, which collected computer inputs such as mouse movements and keystrokes. The tool was initially mandatory, but employees were later given a limited ability to opt out. The program was intended to train AI systems to operate computer software like humans.
   The internal security issue was discovered on June 18 and addressed within four hours, but the initial fix didn't stick, and access to the data had to be further locked down. 
   The pause of the program was announced by Stephane Kasriel, a Meta vice president overseeing AI research, who stated that the company would only re-enable the program when confident in the effectiveness of its data protection controls.
   ## Why it matters
   The pause of the employee-tracking program highlights the concerns over employee privacy and security. The internal security issue has led to a re-evaluation of the program's effectiveness and the potential risks associated with it. The incident also raises questions about the balance between the need for data to train AI systems and the need to protect employee privacy.
