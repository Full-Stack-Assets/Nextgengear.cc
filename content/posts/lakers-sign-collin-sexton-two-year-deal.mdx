The Los Angeles Lakers have made a significant move in free agency, securing guard Collin Sexton on a two-year, $19 million deal. This signing, utilizing the Room Exception, caps off a busy period for the Lakers that saw them acquire Walker Kessler and sign other key players. Sexton's arrival addresses a clear need for additional scoring, playmaking, and rim pressure in the backcourt, aiming to bolster the team's offensive capabilities and depth for the upcoming season. It signals the Lakers' commitment to retooling their roster with younger, athletic talent.

<Callout type="takeaway">The Lakers' acquisition of Collin Sexton provides a much-needed injection of scoring and rim pressure, completing their spending power for the offseason.</Callout>

## What happened
The Lakers officially agreed to a two-year, $19 million contract with Collin Sexton, finalizing a flurry of free agency moves that included a trade for Walker Kessler and deals with Sandro Mamukelashvili and Quentin Grimes. This signing utilized the team's Room Exception, indicating that the Lakers are now limited to veteran minimum deals for any further roster additions. Interest in Sexton from the Lakers has been long-standing, with the opportunity finally materializing this offseason.

Sexton, 27, brings a dynamic scoring presence to Los Angeles. Last season, splitting time between the Hornets and Bulls, he averaged 15.4 points and 3.3 assists, shooting 48.5% from the field and an impressive 40.1% from beyond the arc. His career 3-point percentage stands at 38.9%, consistently above 39% since the 2022-23 season. Known for his ability to attack the rim, 27.5% of his shots last season were within three feet, where he converted at a 64.7% clip, a crucial skill the Lakers previously relied heavily on from Luka Dončić and Austin Reaves.

## Why it matters
This signing is significant for the Lakers as it addresses critical gaps in their backcourt rotation following the departures of Marcus Smart and Luke Kennard. Sexton's offensive skillset, particularly his scoring and ability to create rim pressure, offers a vital complement to Luka Dončić and Austin Reaves. By adding a player who can consistently get to the basket and shoot efficiently from three, the Lakers aim to diversify their offensive attack and reduce the burden on their primary ball-handlers.

The deal also reflects the Lakers' strategic shift towards a younger, more athletic roster. At 27, Sexton fits this mold, providing both immediate impact and potential for growth. His prior experience playing alongside Walker Kessler in Utah could also facilitate quicker chemistry integration, which is crucial given the number of new pieces on the team. However, the use of the Room Exception means the Lakers are now hard-capped, limiting their flexibility for future high-value signings and forcing them to rely on minimum contracts to fill out the roster.

While the financial implications of the Room Exception are restrictive, Sexton's age and skill set offer a clear upside for a team looking to get younger and more dynamic.
