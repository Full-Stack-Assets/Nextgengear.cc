The Trump administration's restrictions on a popular student-loan-forgiveness program for public servants have been struck down by two federal judges. The program, known as Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF), cancels the education debt of government and nonprofit employees after 10 years of service and 120 monthly loan payments. This ruling is significant because it affects over a million Americans who have benefited from the program. 

<Callout type="takeaway"> The Trump administration's restrictions on loan forgiveness for public servants have been struck down by federal judges, allowing the program to continue without the new eligibility requirements.</Callout>

## What happened
Two federal judges, U.S. District Judge Myong J. Joun in Massachusetts and U.S. District Judge Amir Ali in Washington, D.C., ruled in favor of advocates who challenged the Trump administration's revised eligibility requirements for PSLF. The judges found that the new rules overstepped the agency's power and threatened to violate First Amendment protections for free speech. The Trump administration had moved to add new eligibility rules that would strip the benefit from workers whose employers are deemed to have a "substantial illegal purpose."

The overhaul targeted nonprofits and government organizations that support causes at odds with the Trump administration's priorities. It gave the education secretary power to exclude groups from the program if they engage in certain activities. The rules were set to take effect the day after the judges' rulings. 

The judges' decisions were in response to lawsuits filed by more than 20 states, along with a coalition of nonprofit groups and cities. The rulings came after the Department of Education failed to connect its definitions of illegal activity to criminal statutes. 

## Why it matters
The ruling is a significant victory for public servants and nonprofit organizations that rely on the PSLF program to attract college graduates to jobs that traditionally pay less than the private sector. The program has canceled loans for over a million Americans and is an important benefit for workers in government and nonprofit jobs. The Trump administration's overhaul of the program would have undercut this benefit and made it more difficult for public servants to have their loans forgiven.
