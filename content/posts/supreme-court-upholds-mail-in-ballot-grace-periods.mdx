In a significant ruling, the Supreme Court recently voted to block the Republican National Committee's attempt to limit the window for counting absentee ballots in federal elections. This decision, authored by Justice Amy Coney Barrett, affirms states' constitutional authority to establish grace periods for ballots postmarked by Election Day but received afterward. While seen as a commonsense upholding of existing practices, the ruling arrives amidst a backdrop of persistent political challenges to mail-in voting, particularly from figures like former President Donald Trump, who has frequently questioned the method's integrity.

<Callout type="takeaway">
The Supreme Court's ruling solidifies states' authority to establish grace periods for mail-in ballots, yet it won't prevent continued political challenges to voting processes.
</Callout>

## What happened

The Supreme Court's 5-4 decision rejected a challenge brought by the Republican National Committee (RNC) and the Mississippi State GOP, which sought to overturn Mississippi's law allowing a five-day grace period for ballots postmarked by Election Day. The plaintiffs argued that this grace period ignored federal laws setting Election Day as a fixed date and claimed that mail-in voting disproportionately benefited Democrats, citing data from the MIT Election Lab suggesting a higher percentage of Democratic voters used mail-in ballots in 2022.

Justice Amy Coney Barrett, writing for the majority, found that federal law does not override states' constitutional role in administering elections. Her opinion clarified that the definition of "election" does not inherently require all votes to be received and tallied by the close of Election Day. This ruling effectively overturned a prior decision by the deeply conservative 5th Circuit Court of Appeals, which had initially sided with the GOP's arguments. The legal challenge echoed a similar attempt by Pennsylvania Republicans in 2020 to invalidate ballots received after Election Day, which a deadlocked Supreme Court ultimately allowed to be counted.

## Why it matters

This Supreme Court decision is crucial for several reasons. It provides legal clarity and reinforces the long-standing principle of states' autonomy in managing their election processes, particularly concerning the logistical aspects of ballot counting. By upholding grace periods, the ruling helps protect voter access, ensuring that ballots cast legally and on time are not disenfranchised due to postal delays or other administrative factors beyond a voter's control. This is especially significant given the increasing reliance on mail-in voting in many states, with some, like California, seeing over 80% of ballots cast by mail.

However, the ruling's legal finality does not necessarily translate into an end to political contention. Former President Trump has a history of fluctuating stances on mail-in voting, often raising concerns about its legitimacy, particularly after the "red mirage" phenomenon in 2020 where initial in-person vote counts shifted as mail-in ballots were tabulated. Despite the Supreme Court's definitive stance, the decision is unlikely to deter continued rhetoric and efforts to reshape voting laws through legislative means, such as the proposed SAVE America Act, which aims to make mail-in voting more difficult.

## How to think about it

When considering this Supreme Court decision, it's important to distinguish between legal precedent and ongoing political narratives. Legally, the ruling offers a strong affirmation of states' ability to manage their election timelines, particularly regarding the receipt of mail-in ballots. For voters, this means that if your state has a grace period for postmarked ballots, that policy is now on firmer legal ground. However, politically, the debate over mail-in voting is deeply entrenched and will likely continue. Voters should remain informed about their specific state's election laws and be wary of claims that misrepresent the legal status or integrity of mail-in ballots.
