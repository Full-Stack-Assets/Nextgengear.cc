The recent introduction of the Google Home Speaker, featuring enhanced Gemini AI capabilities, aimed to redefine the smart home experience. However, for users accustomed to devices with integrated displays, the transition from a visual assistant like the Google Pixel Tablet to a screen-less speaker has highlighted a significant gap in functionality. This shift reveals that for many everyday smart home tasks, the absence of a visual interface can be a major drawback, impacting usability and convenience in unexpected ways.

<Callout type="takeaway">Despite its advanced Gemini AI, the new Google Home Speaker's lack of a display significantly hinders its utility for essential smart home functions like managing multiple timers, a feature where devices like the Pixel Tablet excel.</Callout>

## What happened
The Google Pixel Tablet, when docked, served effectively as a kitchen smart home assistant, particularly praised for its ability to display and manage multiple timers. Its lock screen provided a constant time display, and the visual countdowns were crucial for cooking and other time-sensitive tasks, allowing users to quickly check progress without verbal interaction.

In contrast, the new Google Home Speaker, despite its compact design and Gemini integration, lacks any visual display. While it can set timers verbally, the inability to see a countdown or the current time proves to be a significant inconvenience. Users found themselves frequently asking Gemini for remaining time, a process that is slower and less intuitive than a quick glance at a screen. Attempts to get Gemini to provide visual or audible countdowns for specific segments of a timer were unsuccessful, forcing users to rely on their smartphones for visual timer management.

## Why it matters
This situation highlights a fundamental design choice in smart home devices: the trade-off between a sleek, screen-less form factor and practical utility. For environments like the kitchen, where quick visual information is paramount for tasks such as cooking or managing schedules, a display-equipped device offers a significant advantage. The reliance on purely verbal interaction, even with advanced AI like Gemini, can introduce friction and inefficiency into routines that were previously streamlined by visual cues.

Furthermore, Google's current smart display lineup presents a challenge. The highly functional Pixel Tablet and its dock are discontinued, pushing users towards the used market. While the Nest Hub (2nd Gen) and Nest Hub Max are technically current, their availability can be inconsistent. This scarcity of readily available Google-branded smart displays with robust timer functionality could lead users to explore alternatives, such as Amazon's Echo Show devices, which continue to offer screen-based smart assistant experiences at various price points.

## How to think about it
When choosing a smart home assistant, prioritize your primary use cases. If your daily routine involves frequent multitasking, cooking, or managing schedules where glanceable information is critical, a device with a display is likely indispensable. While advanced AI like Gemini offers powerful conversational abilities, it cannot fully replicate the efficiency of visual feedback for tasks like tracking multiple timers. Evaluate whether a purely auditory interface will genuinely enhance or complicate your interactions, especially for time-sensitive activities.

<Callout type="warning">Do not assume that the advanced conversational capabilities of Gemini AI can fully compensate for the absence of a visual display in a smart home speaker, especially if you rely on quick, glanceable information for tasks like managing timers or checking the time.</Callout>
