Newcastle United invested heavily last summer, but not all their big signings immediately delivered the expected impact. While players like Alexander Isak and Bruno Guimaraes quickly became successes, the club's £100m-plus net spend saw mixed returns from its latest recruits. However, recent performances at the World Cup by Anthony Elanga and Yoane Wissa have offered a glimmer of hope. Their combined five goals at the tournament matched their entire tally from their first season at St James' Park, raising questions about whether this renewed confidence can translate into a stronger second campaign for the Magpies.

## What happened
Anthony Elanga, signed from Nottingham Forest for £55m, and Yoane Wissa, also a £55m acquisition from Brentford, both exited the World Cup in the last 32, but not without making an impression. Elanga, representing Sweden, scored four minutes after coming on as a substitute against the Netherlands and followed up with another goal as a starter against Japan. Despite Sweden's eventual exit to France, his rapid forward play offered a clear demonstration of his capabilities.

Wissa, playing for DR Congo, also found the net at the tournament. Both players collectively scored five goals at the World Cup, mirroring their total output during their entire debut season with Newcastle. This performance contrasts sharply with their initial struggles to adapt to Newcastle's detailed system, with Elanga facing challenges in consistency and Wissa experiencing a disrupted pre-season and a knee injury after pushing for his move.

## Why it matters
For Newcastle United, the improved form of Elanga and Wissa is crucial. The club needs more from its existing squad, especially given the significant investment made last summer. Head coach Eddie Howe has expressed belief in these signings, and their World Cup displays could be the confidence boost needed to unlock their potential. If these players can carry their international momentum back to Tyneside, it could significantly bolster Newcastle's attacking options and justify the substantial transfer fees.

The situation also highlights the adaptation period many players face, even those with prior Premier League experience like Anthony Gordon, who took time to flourish at Newcastle after joining in 2023. Gordon's success after a confidence-boosting tournament with England Under-21s provides a blueprint for Elanga and Wissa. Their ability to deliver consistently will impact Newcastle's squad depth and overall performance, particularly as the club seeks reinforcements and aims to compete at a high level.

## How to think about it
When evaluating player performance after a major international tournament, it's important to consider the context. While a strong World Cup can be a significant confidence booster and showcase individual talent, the tactical demands and team dynamics at club level can differ greatly. For Elanga and Wissa, the challenge will be translating their individual flashes of brilliance into consistent contributions within Eddie Howe's specific system. Fans and analysts should look for sustained effort, improved decision-making in the final third, and better integration with teammates rather than just isolated goal tallies. Their second season at Newcastle will be a critical period for proving their long-term value.

<Callout type="warning">While encouraging, it's crucial not to overstate the immediate impact of World Cup performances. Players often face different tactical roles and team dynamics at their clubs, and consistency over a full league season is a far greater test.</Callout>
