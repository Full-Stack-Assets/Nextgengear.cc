The Anaheim Ducks have made a significant move ahead of NHL free agency, signing forward A.J. Greer to a four-year, $17 million contract with an average annual value (AAV) of $4.25 million. This deal comes swiftly after the Ducks acquired Greer's rights from the Florida Panthers in exchange for defenseman Radko Gudas. The signing has immediately sparked debate among analysts, with many questioning the long-term value and timing of such a substantial commitment to a role player. It sets an intriguing tone for the Ducks' offseason as they navigate their rebuilding phase and prepare for future cap challenges.

<Callout type="takeaway">The Anaheim Ducks' four-year, $17 million contract for A.J. Greer represents a significant overpayment for a bottom-six forward, potentially impacting future cap flexibility.</Callout>

## What happened
The Anaheim Ducks officially signed A.J. Greer to a four-year contract on Tuesday, carrying a $4.25 million annual cap hit through the 2029-30 season. This $17 million deal includes a 10-team no-trade clause for its entire duration. The signing occurred just one day after the Ducks secured Greer's rights from the Florida Panthers, trading defenseman Radko Gudas's rights in return.

Greer, 29, is coming off a career-best season with the Panthers, where he recorded 17 goals and 15 assists for 32 points in 78 games, along with 113 penalty minutes. This surpassed his previous career high in points. Over nine NHL seasons, he has accumulated 36 goals and 45 assists for 81 points in 326 regular-season games across four different teams.

## Why it matters
While the Ducks currently possess ample cap space, approximately $40 million after this signing, the contract is widely viewed as an overpayment for a player of Greer's projected role. Analysts point to his career-high production last season, noting his shooting percentage jumped to 16 percent from a career average of 10 percent, raising questions about the sustainability of his offensive output. Committing $4.25 million annually to a player who historically performs at a fourth-line value could become problematic as the team's promising entry-level talent, like Leo Carlsson and Cutter Gauthier, approach their own significant contract extensions.

The timing of this deal is particularly scrutinized, as future long-term contracts for Carlsson and Gauthier are projected to command a collective $20 million cap hit, leaving less flexibility for other key signings, particularly on defense. General managers are often cautioned against committing substantial money and term, especially with trade protection, to role players during the early stages of a team's playoff window. This move could limit the Ducks' ability to pursue star talent or maintain cost-effective depth in later years, affecting the team's overall competitive strategy.

## How to think about it
For Ducks fans, this signing should be viewed as a short-term gamble on a player who showed flashes of higher potential last season, balanced against the long-term strategic implications. While Greer offers a physical, forechecking presence that can contribute to a competitive fourth line, the financial commitment and term carry significant risk. It's crucial to monitor how his performance aligns with the $4.25 million AAV over the next four years, especially as the team's younger stars mature and require substantial extensions. The success of this contract will largely depend on whether Greer can consistently deliver value beyond his historical output and if the Ducks can still build a championship-contending roster around their core without being constrained by this deal.

<Callout type="warning">Ducks management should be cautious about setting a precedent of overpaying role players, as this strategy can quickly erode future cap flexibility and hinder the team's ability to acquire or retain top-tier talent as their competitive window opens.</Callout>
