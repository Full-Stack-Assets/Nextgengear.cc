In a stark illustration of the climate crisis, a London Climate Action Week event titled "Extreme Heat: Improving Governance and Strengthening Action Around the World" was abruptly canceled due to an extreme heat warning issued by the UK Met Office. The irony of a conference focused on mitigating and adapting to rising temperatures being thwarted by the very phenomenon it aimed to address was not lost on organizers. This incident highlights the immediate and tangible challenges faced by unprepared urban environments globally, even as experts gather to discuss solutions.

<Callout type="takeaway">
The cancellation of an extreme heat conference due to actual extreme heat underscores the immediate and growing challenge of climate adaptation, especially in unprepared urban environments.
</Callout>

## What happened
The event, hosted by the LSE’s Grantham Research Institute on Climate Change and the Environment in collaboration with the Zurich Climate Resilience Alliance, was scheduled to open with an award announcement and then delve into improving global extreme heat governance and action. However, a rare red weather warning for extreme heat, covering a significant portion of southern England including London, forced its cancellation. Organizers cited the lack of adequate cooling mechanisms in the venue, typical for many London buildings, and the unacceptable risk to the well-being of speakers and guests during intense heat.

The Met Office's warning came as a "heat dome" settled over Western Europe, with temperatures in London forecast to approach 40°C, potentially surpassing the UK’s June record of 35.6°C. This decision to cancel, while pragmatic, inadvertently underscored the very urgency the conference aimed to convey, demonstrating how existing infrastructure is ill-equipped for the escalating climate reality.

## Why it matters
This cancellation serves as a potent reminder that many regions, particularly in Europe, are grappling with infrastructure not designed for the current climate reality. Buildings constructed decades or even centuries ago predate the widespread need for air conditioning, making them vulnerable to sustained high temperatures. Experts warn that heatwaves will become more frequent and intense as long as emissions continue, with one professor noting that temperatures above 35°C, once rare in the UK, have occurred in seven of the last twelve years.

The implications extend beyond event disruptions. Projections suggest that 92% of UK homes could overheat by the 2050s, posing significant health risks and straining public services. This incident emphasizes the critical need for rapid adaptation strategies, not just for future planning but for immediate implementation, as the impacts of climate change are already being felt in unexpected and disruptive ways.

## How to think about it
Addressing extreme heat requires a comprehensive approach that moves beyond simply installing more air conditioning units. While AC can be necessary for peak temperatures, especially when night temperatures exceed 30°C, it's not a standalone solution. A more sustainable strategy involves redesigning heat management systems, starting with passive measures like mandating external shades to prevent heat from entering buildings and planting trees to combat the urban heat island effect. For existing structures, adapting buildings to be more resilient to heat will take time and careful planning, considering factors like building age, historical preservation, and local regulations. New construction should prioritize designs that inherently manage heat, reflecting current and projected climate realities.

<Callout type="warning">
Do not view air conditioning as the sole or primary solution for extreme heat, particularly in regions with older building stock. Relying entirely on AC can exacerbate energy consumption and emissions, contributing to the problem. Retrofitting existing buildings with AC can be complex due to structural limitations, noise regulations, landlord approvals, and historical preservation laws. Prioritize passive cooling and urban planning solutions first.
</Callout>
