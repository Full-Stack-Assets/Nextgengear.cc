Twin earthquakes of magnitude 7.2 and 7.5 struck Venezuela's northern coast, leading to widespread devastation and a significant humanitarian crisis. Rescue teams have been working tirelessly, pulling 33 survivors from the rubble, including an infant and two 11-year-old children, amidst a rapidly rising death toll of 1,430. The critical 72-hour survival window for those trapped beneath debris has now passed, making the ongoing search for over 68,900 missing individuals increasingly desperate. This disaster highlights the urgent need for international aid and advanced communication solutions in crisis zones.

## What happened
Venezuela's northern coast was decimated by successive magnitude 7.2 and 7.5 earthquakes, resulting in a reported death toll of 1,430 and over 3,000 injured. The worst devastation is concentrated in La Guaira state, where entire buildings collapsed. As of late Saturday, families had listed 68,900 people missing, underscoring the chaos in accounting for those affected and cut off by communication failures.

Over the weekend, search-and-rescue crews, including teams from the U.S. military, successfully pulled 33 people alive from collapsed structures. Among those rescued were an infant, an 11-year-old boy detected 10 feet below the surface by a Colombian team, and another 11-year-old saved by Mexican crews. These efforts unfolded as hundreds of aftershocks continued to rattle damaged neighborhoods, complicating rescue operations.

The critical 72-hour survival window, during which the probability of finding survivors alive drops sharply, passed on Saturday evening. In response to the communication challenges, Starlink Mobile has begun providing free connectivity to MovistarVe customers in the La Guaira region, with plans to extend service to other providers, enabling families and businesses to stay connected via SMS even when terrestrial networks are down.

## Why it matters
The immediate and long-term implications of these earthquakes are profound, affecting tens of thousands of lives. Beyond the tragic loss of life and injuries, approximately 3,000 people are now living in shelters, facing displacement and an uncertain future. The scale of missing persons, estimated at over 68,900, presents an immense challenge for authorities and humanitarian organizations, creating widespread anxiety and grief among families.

This disaster underscores the critical importance of international humanitarian aid and rapid response capabilities. The involvement of various global rescue teams, alongside the U.S. military, highlights the necessity of coordinated efforts in large-scale emergencies. Furthermore, the deployment of satellite communication services like Starlink demonstrates how technology can bridge critical gaps in infrastructure, providing vital connectivity when traditional networks fail, which is crucial for coordinating aid and connecting survivors with loved ones.

The ongoing aftershocks not only hinder rescue efforts but also perpetuate a state of fear and instability for survivors, many of whom remain outside damaged buildings. The sheer number of missing individuals also points to the difficulties in disaster management, from accurate accounting to efficient communication, emphasizing the need for robust preparedness plans and resilient infrastructure in earthquake-prone regions.
