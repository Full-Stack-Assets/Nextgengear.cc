Robert Davi, the 75‑year‑old actor known for "Die Hard," took to X on Friday to denounce New York City Mayor Zohran Mamdani after a city‑released map of immigrant enclaves failed to show Little Italy. The map, which highlighted 30 historic neighborhoods across the five boroughs, omitted the iconic Italian‑American district along with several Jewish and Irish areas. Italian‑American advocacy groups quickly called out the oversight, prompting the mayor’s office to promise an update. The incident has ignited a broader conversation about how civic symbols reflect the city’s diverse heritage.

<Callout type="takeaway">The missing Little Italy on NYC’s immigrant map sparked backlash and forced the city to reconsider how it represents cultural neighborhoods.</Callout>

## What happened
The "New York City Immigrant Enclaves" map resurfaced on social media earlier this week, showcasing neighborhoods such as Chinatown, Harlem and Little Haiti, but conspicuously leaving out Little Italy. Viewers also noted the absence of historically Jewish and Irish districts, despite the map’s claim to cover 30 communities.

Actor Robert Davi responded with a video, calling Mayor Mamdani a "jerk" and demanding respect for the Italian‑American community that helped build the city. He urged the mayor to "respect the city you're in" and even suggested the mayor should leave the United States.

Following the viral criticism, the mayor’s office announced it would revise the map to include Little Italy and the other omitted neighborhoods, acknowledging the oversight.

## Why it matters
Civic representations like official maps shape public perception of a city’s identity. Excluding a well‑known enclave can be seen as erasing the contributions of its residents, fueling feelings of marginalization among Italian‑American, Jewish, and Irish communities. The controversy also highlights the political sensitivity surrounding immigrant narratives in a city that prides itself on diversity. For policymakers, the incident serves as a reminder that inclusive branding requires meticulous community consultation.
