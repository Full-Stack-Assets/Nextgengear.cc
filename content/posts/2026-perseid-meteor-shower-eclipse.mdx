The Perseid meteor shower has ignited the night sky as of tonight, ushering in a spectacular display that will culminate on Aug. 12‑13. The shower reaches its peak, with the American Meteor Society estimating up to 100 meteors per hour. The peak aligns with a total solar eclipse, offering a rare double‑dose of celestial drama. For anyone with a dark horizon, the timing presents one of the season’s best stargazing opportunities.

<Callout type="takeaway">The 2026 Perseids deliver a peak of roughly 100 meteors per hour, timed with a total solar eclipse.</Callout>

## What happened
The Perseid stream, debris from comet Swift‑Tuttle, begins sweeping over Earth tonight and will stay active through the first half of August. The shower’s radiant sits near the red supergiant Eta Persei in Perseus, and the peak night of Aug 12‑13 falls under a new‑moon sky, eliminating lunar glare that hampered the 2025 display. The American Meteor Society reports potential rates of up to 100 meteors per hour during the core hours.

Observers in the United States can start looking after 10 p.m. local time, with the highest counts occurring in the pre‑dawn hours when the radiant climbs to its highest point. To maximize sightings, viewers should face the north‑western horizon, locate the radiant about 40° above it, and stay at least 20 minutes for eye adaptation. The shower is also known for vivid green fireballs that can travel up to 37 mi/s (59 km/s).

The total solar eclipse on Aug 12‑13 will trace a narrow path of totality across the continent; anyone inside that path who also sees a bright Perseid fireball will witness a fleeting flash that briefly outshines the eclipsed Sun, though such coincidences are rare.

## Why it matters
The convergence of a high‑rate Perseid peak with a total solar eclipse creates a unique observational window for both amateur and professional astronomers. With lunar illumination absent, the sky will be darker than in recent years, allowing fainter meteors and spectacular green fireballs to be seen. This event also offers educators a dramatic example of how comet debris and solar eclipses can intersect, sparking public interest in night‑sky science.

## How to think about it
Choose a location with minimal light pollution and a clear view of the north‑western horizon. Arrive at least 30 minutes before darkness to let your eyes adapt, and bring a reclining chair or blanket for comfort. Use a star‑mapping app to locate the Perseid radiant near Eta Persei, then scan the sky patiently, especially during the hours before dawn. If you’re within the eclipse’s path of totality, wear certified solar glasses for the partial phases and switch to naked‑eye viewing once totality begins.

<Callout type="warning">Never look directly at the Sun during the eclipse without proper solar filters; use eclipse glasses or indirect viewing methods.</Callout>
